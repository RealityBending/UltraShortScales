# Compendium of Ultra-Short Psychology Questionnaires

> **Contribute**: Know of any well-established short questionnaires? Share the reference!

## Clinical, Health, and Well-being

### Symptoms, Screening, and Health
#### Clinical symptom screening
* **PHQ-4** *(4 items)*: Depression and anxiety. PHQ-2 (depression) + GAD-2 (anxiety). Widely used, excellent screening properties.
* **CDS-2** *(2 items)*: Cambridge Depersonalization Scale (2-item version). Michal et al. (2011).
* **Bubbles-DASS** *(3×1 items)*: One-item screens for depression, anxiety, and stress based on the DASS-21 subscales. In two large German samples, Brailovskaia et al. (2024) showed that each single-item “bubble” closely matched the corresponding DASS-21 subscale, with factor-level fit and expected correlations with other mental-health variables. The Depression-, Anxiety-, and Stress-Bubble items can be used as ultra-brief screeners in research or clinical settings.
* **PTSD-PCL-2** *(2 items)*: A 2-item ultra-short Posttraumatic Stress Disorder Checklist (PCL-2). Prior work has found that 2-item and 6-item PCL versions serve as effective PTSD screens in primary care. For example, Lang et al. (2012) report that abbreviated PCL versions (including a 2-item form) maintain high sensitivity and correlate strongly with the full 17-item PCL, making PCL-2 a practical ultra-short PTSD screener.

#### Health, stress, and sleep
* **SQS** *(1 item)*: Single-Item Sleep Quality Scale. Snyder et al. (2018). Possesses excellent concurrent criterion validity and tracks well with longer scales like the Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI) while significantly reducing participant burden in daily diary or clinical trials. 
    * *"During the past 7 days, how would you rate your sleep quality overall?" (Scored on a 0–10 scale, where 0 = terrible and 10 = excellent)*.
* **SIMS** *(1 item)*: Single-Item Measure of Stress Symptoms. Elo et al. (2003). Widely used in occupational health and population studies; validated extensively in Scandinavian public health tracking and clinical populations.
    * *"Stress means a situation in which a person feels tense, restless, nervous or anxious or is unable to sleep at night because his/her mind is troubled all the time. Do you feel this kind of stress these days?" (Scored on a 1 to 5 Likert scale from "not at all" to "very much")*.
* **SRH** *(1 item)*: Self-Rated Health. DeSalvo et al. (2006) / Idler & Benyamini (1997). Perhaps the most thoroughly validated single item in epidemiology and psychology. Consistently shown to be an independent predictor of mortality and morbidity, often outperforming comprehensive medical examinations.
    * *"In general, would you say your health is: Excellent, Very good, Good, Fair, or Poor?"*.
* **PHCS-2** *(2 items)*: Perceived Health Competence Scale – Short Form. Original: Smith et al. (1995); Short Form: Ehlers et al. (2025). Assesses domain-specific self-efficacy focused on health management. Highly predictive of behaviors like physical activity and medical rehabilitation enrollment.
    * *1. "It is difficult for me to find effective solutions to the health problems that come my way." (Reverse-scored)*.
    * *2. "I am able to do things for my health as well as most other people."*.

#### Body awareness and interoception
* **BQP-VSF** *(12 items)*: Body Perception Questionnaire – Very Short Form. Brand et al. (2024). *(12 items — borderline; included for completeness.)*.
* **PAQ-S** *(6 items)*: Perth Alexithymia Questionnaire – Short Form. Preece et al. (2023).

### Self-Regulation and Coping
#### Emotion reactivity and emotion regulation
* **B-ERS** *(6 items)*: Brief Emotion Reactivity Scale. Veilleux et al. (2024).
* **DERS-SSF** *(6 items)*: Difficulties in Emotion Regulation Scale – Super Short Form. Original English development: Kaufman et al. (2016); French validation: Dan-Glauser & Quartier.
* **ERQ-S** *(6 items)*: Emotion Regulation Questionnaire – Short Form. Valenti & Faraci (2025).
* **CERQ-Short** *(18 items)*: Cognitive Emotion Regulation Questionnaire – Short Form. Original: Garnefski & Kraaij (2006). Recent validation: Saetren et al. (2024).

#### Coping, defences, and psychological flexibility
* **Brief COPE** *(28 items)*: (Carver, 1997). This is the absolute gold standard for your criteria. It contains 28 items measuring 14 distinct coping dimensions (e.g., Active Coping, Denial, Use of Emotional Support). It features exactly 2 items per dimension.
* **DSQ-40** *(40 items)*: Defense Style Questionnaire. While 40 items might sound long, it measures 20 specific defense mechanisms (e.g., projection, acting out, humor, sublimation). This means there are exactly 2 items per defense mechanism. These 20 mechanisms then roll up into three broader styles: Mature, Neurotic, and Immature.
* **CompACT-8** *(8 items)*: Comprehensive assessment of Acceptance and Commitment Therapy processes. An 8-item highly validated brief scale that measures three dimensions of psychological flexibility (Openness to Experience, Behavioral Awareness, Valued Action), yielding roughly 2 to 3 items per dimension.
* **AAQ-II** *(7 items)*: Acceptance and Action Questionnaire. The standard 7-item measure for experiential avoidance and psychological inflexibility. It operates as a single-dimension scale.
* **CD-RISC-2** *(2 items)*: Connor-Davidson Resilience Scale 2. Vaishnavi et al. (2007). Derived from the 25-item original, this is an extremely popular, heavily validated measure of "bounce-back" adaptability that demonstrates excellent test-retest reliability and convergent validity.
    * *1. "I am able to adapt when changes occur."*.
    * *2. "I tend to bounce back after illness, injury, or other hardships."*.
    * *(Scored on a 5-point scale from 0 = not true at all to 4 = true nearly all the time)*.

#### Uncertainty, ambiguity, control, and mindset
* **IUS-12** *(12 items)*: Intolerance of Uncertainty Scale - Short Form (Carleton et al., 2007). A 12-item scale that measures two dimensions: Prospective Anxiety (7 items) and Inhibitory Anxiety (5 items).
* **MSTAT-II** *(13 items)*: Multiple Stimulus Types Ambiguity Tolerance. A 13-item unidimensional scale that is significantly shorter and more modern than the classic Budner scale.
* **Brief IPC Scale** *(9 items)*: Locus of Control (Sapp & Harrod, 1993). Rotter's original scale is quite long. This validated brief version of Levenson's IPC scale is only 9 items. It measures three dimensions: Internal, Powerful Others, and Chance. This gives you exactly 3 items per dimension.
* **Growth Mindset Scale** *(3 items)*: A brief version of Dweck’s mindset measure. Rammstedt & Grüning (2023) adapted the 3-item Growth Mindset scale for German adolescents and adults, finding it “sufficiently reliable” with high internal consistency (α ≈ .83–.91 across groups). They also derived a single-item growth mindset measure, but the 3-item scale provides better reliability while still being very brief.

### Subjective Well-Being and Quality of Life
#### Subjective well-being and quality of life
* **SILS** *(1 item)*: Single-Item Life Satisfaction Scale. Jovanović & Lazić (2020). Jovanović et al. (2020).
    * *"All things considered, how satisfied are you with your life as a whole?"* (0 = totally dissatisfied to 10 = totally satisfied).
* **SHS-1** *(1 item)*: Single-Item Happiness Scale. Abdel-Khalek (2006). Demonstrated high test-retest reliability (.86) and strong concurrent validity with the Oxford Happiness Inventory and the subjective well-being literature.
    * *"In general, how happy or unhappy do you usually feel?" (Scored on an 11-point scale from 0 = extremely unhappy to 10 = extremely happy)*.
* **Essential QoL-3** *(3 items)*: A newly validated ultra-brief quality-of-life scale covering global life satisfaction, happiness, and meaningfulness. Developed by Schumann et al. (2023), the Essential QoL-3 was shown to validly assess core well-being dimensions in large surveys.
* **“Positive bubbles”** *(3×1 items)*: One-item “bubble” scales for positive mental health, life satisfaction, and perceived social support. Brailovskaia & Margraf (2024) validated single-item versions derived from the PMH-Scale, Satisfaction with Life Scale, and Social Support Questionnaire. Each bubble scale had excellent fit and reliability, tracking the full-scale scores and expected correlates, making them useful ultra-brief well-being screens.



## Personality, Cognition, and Individual Differences

### Basic Personality Trait Models
#### Basic trait models: Big Five
* **TIPI** *(10 items)*: Ten-Item Personality Inventory. Gosling et al. (2003). Strong convergence with full Big Five; note Chapman et al. (2017) on limitations at the facet level.
* **BFI-10** *(10 items)*: Big Five Inventory – 10 items. Rammstedt & John (2007). Recent cross-national validation: Mastrascusa et al. (2023).
* **BFI-2-XS** *(15 items)*: Big Five Inventory-2 Extra-Short Form. Soto & John (2017). 3 items per domain, derived from the full BFI-2's facet structure; better content validity than TIPI/BFI-10. Does not support facet-level scoring. Recommended over TIPI/BFI-10 when space allows. German validation available (Danner et al.).
* **FIPI** *(5 items)*: Five-Item Personality Inventory (single item per Big Five trait). Gosling et al. (2003). Useful for extreme brevity (mean r ≈ .65 with longer scales). Each item is preceded by the prompt *"I see myself as:"* and scored on a 7-point Likert scale (1 = disagree strongly to 7 = agree strongly).
    * *1. Extraverted, enthusiastic (that is, sociable, assertive, talkative, active, NOT reserved or shy)*.
    * *2. Agreeable, kind (that is, trusting, generous, sympathetic, cooperative, NOT aggressive or cold)*.
    * *3. Dependable, organized (that is, hard-working, responsible, self-disciplined, thorough, NOT careless or impulsive)*.
    * *4. Emotionally stable, calm (that is, relaxed, self-confident, NOT anxious, moody, easily upset, or easily stressed)*.
    * *5. Open to experience, imaginative (that is, curious, reflective, creative, deep, open-minded, NOT conventional)*.
* **SIMP** *(5 items)*: Single-Item Measures of Personality (bipolar adjective pairs). Woods & Hampson (2005). Mean convergence r ≈ .61.
* **Mini-IPIP** *(20 items)*: Big Five; Donnellan et al. (2006).

#### Basic trait models: HEXACO
* **BHI** *(24 items)*: Brief HEXACO Inventory. De Vries (2013). 4 items per HEXACO domain (1 item per facet). Low alpha reliability but good test–retest stability, self-other agreement, and convergent validity with full HEXACO-PI-R. Recommended when all 6 HEXACO dimensions (including Honesty-Humility) must be covered efficiently.
* **HEX-ACO-18** *(18 items)*: Age-Invariant HEXACO Short Scale. Olaru & Jankowsky (2022). Developed using ant colony optimization (ACO); 3 items per domain, scalar measurement invariance across ages 16–90. Recommended for developmental and lifespan research.

### Maladaptive and Antagonistic Traits
#### Maladaptive, antagonistic, and dark personality
* **PID-5-BF** *(25 items)*: Personality Inventory for DSM-5 – Brief Form. Krueger, Derringer, Markon, Watson, & Skodol (2013; APA); construct validation: Anderson et al. (2018). 5 items × 5 domains (Negative Affectivity, Detachment, Antagonism, Disinhibition, Psychoticism). Operationalizes the DSM-5 Section III Alternative Model for Personality Disorders. Good 5-factor structure and criterion validity; internal consistencies modest but acceptable. Free for research use from APA.
* **SINS** *(1 item)*: Single Item Narcissism Scale. Konrath et al. (2014).
    * *"To what extent do you agree with this statement: I am a narcissist. (Note: The word 'narcissist' means egotistical, self-focused, and vain.)."*.
* **uSDT** *(9 items)*: Ultra-Short Dark Triad (3 items × Machiavellianism, Narcissism, Psychopathy). Wehner et al. (2021). Best choice when strict brevity is needed for all three dark traits.
* **Dirty Dozen (DD)** *(12 items)*: Dark Triad. Jonason & Webster (2010). 4 items × 3 dark traits. Widely cited (1,300+ times); note weaker convergent validity for psychopathy relative to longer measures. Test-retest reliability adequate (median r_tt ≈ .87). Use uSDT if tighter length is needed; DD if slightly more coverage is preferred.
* **SD3** *(27 items)*: Short Dark Triad. Jones & Paulhus (2014). 9 items × 3 dark traits. Most widely used Dark Triad measure overall (1,400+ citations); better construct coverage than DD, especially for psychopathy. Above the ultra-short threshold but essential reference for the literature.
* **A-US** *(3 items)*: Authoritarianism – Ultra Short. Heller et al. (2020).
* **SD4-VSF** *(16 items)*: Very Short Form of the Short Dark Tetrad. (Dark Tetrad: Machiavellianism, Narcissism, Psychopathy, Sadism). Akat (2025). Promising new brief derivative of the SD4. Early evidence is encouraging, but replication is still limited.

### Meaning, Cognition, and Self-Orientation
#### Meaning and coherence
* **MLQ** *(10 items)*: Meaning in Life Questionnaire (Steger et al., 2006). This is perfectly aligned with your needs. It is exactly 10 items total, divided cleanly into two dimensions: Presence of Meaning (5 items) and Search for Meaning (5 items).
* **BASOC** *(3 items)*: Brief Assessment of Sense of Coherence. A very short SOC measure. Schumann et al. (2003) found the original 3-item SOC-3 performed poorly, then developed the 3-item BASOC. They reported that BASOC (items drawn from the full SOC-29) “outperformed the SOC-3” in reliability and validity, and recommend it as a brief SOC measure. BASOC thus offers an ultra-short way to tap Antonovsky’s coherence construct in large surveys.

#### Cognitive style and attention
* **MWQ** *(5 items)*: Mind Wandering Questionnaire (trait-like). Mrazek et al. (2013).

#### Social status and subjective positioning
* **MacArthur SSS** *(1 item)*: MacArthur Scale of Subjective Social Status. Adler et al. (2000). A foundational 1-item sociodemographic measure in health psychology and epidemiology. It frequently predicts physical and mental health outcomes better than objective SES measures (like actual income or education level).
    * *"Think of this ladder as representing where people stand in our society. At the top of the ladder are the people who are the best off—those who have the most money, the most education and the most respected jobs. At the bottom of the ladder are the people who are the worst off... Where would you place yourself on this ladder?" (Scored visually from 1 to 10 on a drawing of a ladder)*.


## Social, Interpersonal, and Occupational Psychology

### Social, Relational, and Family Functioning
#### Attachment and close relationships
* **RQ** *(4 items)*: Relationship Questionnaire (Bartholomew & Horowitz, 1991). This is the ultimate short scale. It consists of exactly 4 items (one paragraph/item for each of the four attachment styles: Secure, Dismissing, Preoccupied, Fearful).
* **ECR-S** *(12 items)*: Experiences in Close Relationships - Short Form (Wei et al., 2007). If you prefer the ECR framework, this version is 12 items total (6 for Anxiety, 6 for Avoidance). Note: The "Anxiety" dimension specifically measures the fear of abandonment.
* **FICQ** *(10 items)*: Fear of Intimacy Components Questionnaire (Recent validation). A validated 10-item short form. It measures two specific dimensions: Fear of Losing the Self (5 items) and Fear of Losing the Other (5 items).

#### Loneliness, belonging, and social media-related social threat
* **TILS/UCLA-3** *(3 items)*: Three-Item UCLA Loneliness Scale. A widely used 3-item loneliness measure. Hughes et al. (2004) showed that TILS has good internal consistency (α ≈ 0.72) and correlates strongly (r ≈ 0.82) with the full UCLA 20-item scale. This ultra-short loneliness scale is validated across many languages and contexts (high convergent validity with depression, social support, etc.), and is suitable for large-scale surveys or when brevity is required.
* **FoMO-3** *(3 items)*: A concise 3-item Fear of Missing Out scale. Hisham et al. (2025) developed FoMO-3 and found it consistently reliable (high internal consistency) in both trait and intensive longitudinal designs. The FoMO-3 showed strict measurement invariance (by sex and time) and correlated as expected with related constructs (e.g. problematic smartphone use), demonstrating that it “can capture the FoMO construct with strong reliability and validity” despite its brevity.

#### Family, caregiving, and cultural/interpersonal style
* **SCCS** *(9 items)*: Short Composite Codependency Scale (Recent validation). This is a newly validated 9-item scale. It breaks codependency down into three dimensions: Emotional Suppression, Interpersonal Control, and Self-Sacrifice. This gives you exactly 3 items per dimension.
* **CHS-SF** *(6 items)*: Cultural Humility Short Form. A 6-item measure (two 3-item subscales: positive humility, negative humility) for therapists’ cultural humility. Coleman et al. (2024) report excellent factor fit and good-to-excellent reliability for the CHS-SF. Like the full Cultural Humility Scale (Hook et al., 2013), the CHS-SF can be used in clinical and training settings to efficiently assess openness to diverse cultural backgrounds.
* **PS-4** *(4 items)*: Parenting Scale – 4 item. An ultra-short version of the Arnold et al. Parenting Scale. Schmalbach et al. (2026) used large German samples to select 4 items (two each for the Overreactivity and Laxness factors). The PS-4 showed excellent model fit and invariance, and its subscales correlated with child behavior measures as expected. The authors recommend the PS-4 for large surveys: it “can be recommended as an ultra-short, yet valid…assessment tool of parenting behavior” when brevity is essential.

### Work and Organisational Functioning
#### Global attitudes and job satisfaction
* **GJS** *(1 item)*: Global Job Satisfaction. Wanous et al. (1997). A landmark meta-analysis (28 correlations, 7,682 individuals) demonstrated that a single-item measure of global job satisfaction strongly correlates (mean r = .67) with multi-item scales, concluding it is highly acceptable and robust for organizational research.
    * *"Overall, how satisfied are you with your job?" (Scored on varying Likert scales)*.

#### Work engagement
* **UWES-3** *(3 items)*: A 3-item version of the Utrecht Work Engagement Scale (one item each for vigor, dedication, absorption). Schaufeli et al. (2019) validated the UWES-3 in five countries (n > 80,000) and found it shares 86–92% of its variance with the 9-item UWES, with very similar correlational patterns to various job outcomes. The authors conclude that “the UWES-3 is a reliable and valid indicator of work engagement” suitable for large surveys where space is limited.

## Notable exclusions (too long, but often confused with ultra-short)
* **HEXACO-60** *(60 items)*: Standard short HEXACO; Ashton & Lee (2009). Use BHI or HEX-ACO-18 instead if time is critical.
* **PID-5-SF** *(100 items)*: Full short-form of PID-5; use PID-5-BF (25 items) for ultra-short applications.
* **BFI-2-S** *(30 items)*: Short form of BFI-2; use BFI-2-XS (15 items) instead.
